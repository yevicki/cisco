Prompt engineering

The art and science of instructing AI models to behave the way you need them to.

Prompt engineering is about shaping how an AI model behaves without retraining it. Rather than modifying the underlying model — an expensive, slow process — you change the instructions the model receives. Three techniques form the foundation of everything built on top of large language models today.

Technique 01
System prompts

Instructions that define the model's role, tone, constraints, and knowledge scope before any user input arrives. The model reads these first, every time.

Technique 02
Few-shot examples

Showing the model 2–5 input/output pairs so it infers the correct pattern — no fine-tuning or retraining required.

Technique 03
Chain-of-thought

Asking the model to reason step-by-step before giving a final answer. Dramatically reduces errors on complex or multi-step tasks.

System prompt — Cisco HR assistant example

System prompt
You are Cisco's HR Policy Assistant. Answer only using the documents provided.
Cite the specific policy section for every factual claim.
If a question is outside your knowledge, say so and direct the employee to HRConnect.
Tone: clear, professional, empathetic.

Few-shot — benefits eligibility classifier

Few-shot examples
Q: "Am I eligible for tuition reimbursement if I started 3 months ago?"
A: No — Policy §7.2 requires 6 months of tenure before tuition benefits apply.

Q: "Can I enroll in dental mid-year?"
A: Only during a qualifying life event (§4.1). Open enrollment is your next opportunity.

Q: [new employee question here]

Chain-of-thought — compensation dispute

Chain-of-thought prompt
Let's work through this step by step.
1. What is the employee's grade band and location?
2. What does the compensation framework say for that band?
3. Does the claimed discrepancy fall outside the published range?
4. What escalation path applies?
Final answer: [conclusion with reasoning]
Key insight

The most powerful property of system prompts for enterprise use: you can update them the moment a policy changes. No engineering sprint, no model retraining. The new context takes effect immediately across every interaction.

Cisco applications

  • Internal HR chatbot: System prompt encodes Cisco's tone guidelines and compliance boundaries. Few-shot examples train it on common benefits Q&A patterns without model retraining.
  • Manager decision support: Chain-of-thought prompts guide managers through structured reasoning on performance reviews, reducing inconsistency across 40,000+ employees globally.
  • Recruiter assist: Few-shot examples show what a strong vs. weak candidate summary looks like — producing consistent output across Cisco's global TA team without baking in criteria explicitly.
  • Policy update rollouts: Update the system prompt when policy changes. No redeployment needed. New context is live immediately.
02

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)

Why grounding AI answers in real documents matters — especially for HR.

RAG solves a fundamental limitation of general-purpose AI: it doesn't know your documents. Rather than hoping the model memorized your benefits guide during training, RAG retrieves the relevant chunks from your own document index first, then passes them to the model as context. The answer is grounded in your actual policies, comes with a citable source, and stays current as long as you keep the index updated.

Employee question → Retrieve relevant docs → Pass to LLM with context → Grounded answer + citation

Without RAG vs. with RAG

Without RAG With RAG
Knowledge source Training data — may be outdated or generic Your actual documents, updated in real time
Hallucination risk High — model fills gaps with plausible-sounding text Low — answers grounded in retrieved chunks
Citations None Specific section reference per answer
Policy updates Requires model retraining Update the document index — done

RAG in action — parental leave question

RAG pipeline walkthrough
Employee: "How many weeks of parental leave do I get?"

Step 1 — Retrieve: system searches Cisco Benefits Guide 2025, §9.3
Step 2 — Context: [relevant policy chunk injected into prompt]
Step 3 — Generate: "Per Cisco's Benefits Guide §9.3, full-time employees
are eligible for 20 weeks of paid parental leave, regardless of gender.
Part-time eligibility is prorated by FTE percentage."

The difference for an employee: instead of "it depends," they get a cited, policy-specific answer in seconds — without opening a ticket.

Why this matters for HR specifically

Wrong HR answers have real consequences — for employees in distress, for legal compliance, for manager decisions. RAG is the mechanism that makes AI answers trustworthy enough to use in consequential contexts.

Cisco HR use cases for RAG

  • Employee Q&A bot: Index Cisco's benefits guides, handbook, and HR FAQs. Employees get instant, cited answers instead of waiting for HR tickets — reducing tier-1 HR volume significantly.
  • Policy search: Replace keyword search on the intranet with semantic retrieval. Employees find the right section even when they don't know the exact terminology.
  • Manager guides: Index playbooks, leadership frameworks, and escalation procedures. Supports consistent decisions across Cisco's distributed leadership team.
  • Global compliance: Separate document indices per jurisdiction (US, EU, APAC). The bot retrieves region-specific policy based on the employee's location — avoiding cross-border compliance errors.
03

AI bias in HR

Why it's regulated, where it surfaces, and how to mitigate it — practically.

AI bias in HR is both a technical and a legal challenge. Bias typically enters through training data that reflects past (and often discriminatory) hiring or promotion patterns, or through proxy variables that correlate with protected characteristics even when those characteristics aren't explicitly included.

Why it's regulated

Title VII, the EEOC's 2023 AI guidance, NYC Local Law 144, and the EU AI Act all treat AI-driven HR decisions as high-risk. Discriminatory output — even unintentional — creates employer liability. NYC LL144 already mandates annual bias audits and candidate disclosure for any automated employment tool used in hiring for NYC-based roles.

"Amazon scrapped an AI recruiting tool after discovering it penalised CVs that contained the word 'women's' — because it was trained on 10 years of predominantly male hire data."

Reuters, 2018 — a foundational case study in AI hiring bias
The core lesson

A model can be technically accurate on its training distribution and still be systematically biased against protected groups. Statistical performance and fairness are not the same thing.

Where bias enters

  • Historical training data: If past hiring decisions were biased (and most were), models trained on that data will reproduce those patterns.
  • Proxy features: Zip code, school name, graduation year, and gap years all correlate with protected class and can act as stand-ins even when race or gender are excluded explicitly.
  • Feedback loops: When a biased model's outputs are used to generate future training data, the bias compounds over time.

Mitigation framework

  • Pre-deployment bias audit: Measure disparate impact across protected groups before any model goes live. Use standard metrics: selection rate ratios, false positive/negative rates by subgroup.
  • Human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions: AI shortlisting, performance scoring, and pay recommendations must have a human reviewer who can override. AI should inform, not decide, on protected-class-adjacent outcomes.
  • Explainability and documentation: Every AI-assisted HR decision needs a documented rationale. This is both a governance best practice and, increasingly, a legal requirement.
  • Ongoing monitoring: Bias can drift as the workforce and world change. Continuous monitoring for outcome disparities, with quarterly reviews and a clear trigger for model retraining or removal.

Cisco context

  • Recruiting automation: Any AI resume screener or interview scorer must be audited before deployment under NYC LL144 for NYC-based roles — and Cisco's global commitments likely extend this further.
  • Performance management: AI-assisted performance scoring must be tested for gender and ethnicity bias across Cisco's 85,000-person workforce before influencing compensation, promotions, or PIPs.
  • Pay equity tools: AI tools that model compensation ranges must be validated for disparate impact — especially given Cisco's public pay equity commitments and EEOC scrutiny.
  • Responsible AI charter: Cisco's existing Responsible AI framework and Ethics team can own the HR bias audit process — aligning AI governance for HR tools with the broader enterprise standard already in place.

The four AI career paths

What the market is actually hiring for in 2026 — and how the roles differ.

The title "Prompt Engineer" has largely evolved into something more substantial. The underlying skills — understanding model behavior, evaluating output quality, designing guardrails — got redistributed into four distinct roles that are growing with real budget behind them.

Think of them as a spectrum from "builds the system" to "judges the system."

Role Core question What you do Technical level
AI Systems Engineer Can I build this? Design and ship RAG pipelines, agentic workflows, API integrations. Closest to a traditional SWE role. Python, vector databases, cloud deployment, LLM APIs all required. High
LLM Quality Specialist Does this work consistently? Design test suites that run model outputs against expected behavior at scale. Sits between product and engineering running systematic evaluations. Requires Python basics and evaluation frameworks. Medium
AI Output Evaluator Is this specific output good? Review actual AI outputs and judge whether they're accurate, fair, on-policy, and appropriate. Often involves human review of flagged outputs that automated testing can't catch. Low–Medium
AI Systems Auditor Is this system legally defensible? Evaluate entire AI pipelines for accuracy, bias, hallucination rates, and compliance with laws like the EU AI Act or NYC LL144. Produces documented audit trails. Medium (governance-heavy)
The governance track is growing fastest

The EU AI Act and US federal AI governance requirements are creating legal demand for documented human oversight in high-risk AI applications. AI Systems Auditor and AI Output Evaluator roles in healthcare, legal, financial services, and enterprise — like Cisco — aren't just business preferences anymore. They're legally required in many contexts.

What the market is actually paying (US, 2026)

  • Junior / no prior AI job: $60,000–$95,000
  • Mid-level (1–2 years experience): $100,000–$160,000
  • Senior AI engineer: $160,000–$250,000+, often with equity at AI startups
  • Average AI engineer salary (2025): $206,000 — up $50,000 year over year

What "domain expertise" actually means

Why your HR background is an asset in AI hiring — not a gap to explain away.

"Domain" means the industry or subject matter the AI is being applied to — not the AI itself. When job listings say "we want domain experts," they mean: we can teach you the LLM tooling, but we can't easily teach you 10 years of knowing how HR, healthcare, finance, or legal actually works.

"Over 75% of AI job listings now ask for domain experts, not generalists. That is why some engineers clear $300K while others stall at half that number."

Second Talent, AI Engineering Skills Report, 2026

Your domain: enterprise HR at scale

Based on this conversation, your domain is HR and enterprise workforce operations. That includes knowing:

  • How HR policies are structured, versioned, and updated
  • What employees actually ask and why — including the emotional context behind questions about FMLA, PIPs, or compensation
  • How employment law interacts with technology decisions — what a wrong answer about FMLA eligibility actually costs
  • The difference between what a manager needs and what an employee needs from the same policy document
  • How HR functions operate at scale — jurisdiction differences, global vs. local policy, escalation paths
The competitive advantage

Most AI engineers can build a RAG pipeline over any document corpus. Very few understand that an HR benefits bot needs to handle jurisdiction-specific policy differences, that certain outputs create legal liability, that employees asking about FMLA or PIP processes are often in distress, or that a wrong answer about compensation has real consequences for a real person. That's what domain expertise provides — and it's genuinely rare in AI hiring right now.

For Cisco specifically, your domain could be positioned as enterprise HR AI for large global workforces — a more defensible and valuable niche than "HR tech" generally, and one where Cisco's scale makes the problems non-trivial.

What to learn next

The concrete technical skills needed to claim this role — in order of priority.

The conceptual knowledge from this guide covers roughly the top of the job description. What the market also expects is the ability to build what you understand. Here's the gap, filled in order of priority.

The technical stack

  • Python at the API-call level — not data science level. Enough to call an API, parse JSON, loop over results, and ship a script. This is the baseline gate for almost every role.
  • Calling the Claude or OpenAI API — handling responses, building a basic multi-turn chat loop, understanding tokens, context windows, and temperature.
  • Vector databases — start with ChromaDB (free, runs locally). Then learn Pinecone or pgvector. Chunking and embedding documents is the core RAG engineering skill.
  • One evaluation framework — Promptfoo or RAGAS are the best starting points. This is the skill that separates "I built a demo" from "I built something production-ready."
  • LangChain or LlamaIndex — orchestration layers. Learn what they abstract away after you've built a RAG pipeline from scratch once, so you understand what's happening underneath.

The portfolio project

Build this one thing

Take 20–50 Cisco HR policy PDFs, chunk them, embed them, build a Q&A bot over them with citations, and deploy it somewhere accessible. That single project demonstrates prompt engineering, RAG architecture, and implicit governance thinking all at once — and it's domain-specific to your actual work context, which is exactly what hiring managers are looking for.

For the governance track specifically

  • Statistical bias metrics — selection rate ratios, disparate impact analysis, false positive rates by subgroup. These are the numbers an audit produces.
  • Explainability tools — SHAP and LIME are the standard libraries for explaining why a model made a particular decision.
  • Regulatory literacy — read the EEOC's 2023 AI guidance, NYC LL144 full text, and the EU AI Act's high-risk system definitions. You don't need a law degree; you need to know what compliance looks like.
  • Audit documentation — understand what an AI bias audit report contains and how to produce one. The NIST AI RMF (Risk Management Framework) is the standard reference.

Learning path (roughly 12–16 weeks)

WeeksFocusOutput
1–2 Python basics + LLM API fundamentals Working chat script that calls Claude or OpenAI
3–4 Embeddings, vector databases, RAG from scratch Q&A system over a folder of HR PDFs with citations
