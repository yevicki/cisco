Prompt engineering basics

The three foundational techniques — and how Cisco could apply them.

Technique 1
System prompts
Instructions that define the model's role, tone, constraints, and knowledge scope before any user input arrives.
Technique 2
Few-shot examples
Showing the model 2–5 input/output pairs so it infers the pattern — no fine-tuning needed.
Technique 3
Chain-of-thought
Asking the model to reason step-by-step before giving a final answer. Reduces errors on complex tasks.
SYSTEM PROMPT — Cisco HR assistant example
You are Cisco's HR Policy Assistant. Answer only using the documents provided. Cite the specific policy section for every factual claim. If a question is outside your knowledge, say so and direct the employee to HRConnect. Tone: clear, professional, empathetic.
FEW-SHOT — Benefits eligibility classifier
Q: "Am I eligible for tuition reimbursement if I started 3 months ago?" A: No — Policy §7.2 requires 6 months of tenure before tuition benefits apply. Q: "Can I enroll in dental mid-year?" A: Only during a qualifying life event (§4.1). Open enrollment is your next opportunity. Q: [new employee question here]
CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT — Compensation dispute
Let's work through this step by step. 1. What is the employee's grade band and location? 2. What does the compensation framework say for that band? 3. Does the claimed discrepancy fall outside the published range? 4. What escalation path applies? Final answer: [conclusion with reasoning]

Cisco applications

Internal HR chatbot

System prompt encodes Cisco's tone guidelines and compliance boundaries. Few-shot examples train it on common benefits Q&A patterns without model retraining.

Manager decision support

Chain-of-thought prompts guide managers through structured reasoning on performance reviews, reducing inconsistency across 40,000+ employees globally.

Recruiter assist

Few-shot examples show what a strong vs. weak candidate summary looks like, producing consistent output across Cisco's global TA team without baking in criteria explicitly.

Policy update rollouts

Update the system prompt when policy changes — no retraining or redeployment. New context takes effect immediately, keeping the assistant accurate post-policy refresh.

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG)

Why grounding AI answers in real documents matters — especially for HR.

Employee question
→
Retrieve relevant docs
→
Pass to LLM with context
→
Grounded answer + citation
Without RAG
Model hallucination risk
The LLM answers from training data — which may be outdated, generic, or simply wrong for Cisco's specific policies.
With RAG
Answers from your docs
The model retrieves chunks from Cisco's own HR documents first, then synthesises an answer — with a citable source.
Key benefit
Always current
Update the document index when policy changes and every answer is immediately up to date — no model retraining.
RAG IN ACTION — Employee asking about parental leave
Employee: "How many weeks of parental leave do I get?" Step 1 — Retrieve: system searches Cisco Benefits Guide 2025, §9.3 Step 2 — Context: [relevant policy chunk injected into prompt] Step 3 — Generate: "Per Cisco's Benefits Guide §9.3, full-time employees are eligible for 20 weeks of paid parental leave, regardless of gender. Part-time eligibility is prorated by FTE percentage."

Cisco HR use cases for RAG

Employee Q&A bot

Index Cisco's benefits guides, handbook, and HR FAQs. Employees get instant, cited answers instead of waiting for HR tickets — reducing tier-1 HR volume significantly.

Policy search

Replace keyword search on the intranet with semantic retrieval. Employees find the right section of the right policy even when they don't know the exact terminology.

AI bias in HR

Why it's regulated, where it surfaces, and how to mitigate it.

Regulated
Why it's a legal issue
Title VII, the EEOC's 2023 AI guidance, NYC Local Law 144, and the EU AI Act all treat AI-driven HR decisions as high-risk. Discriminatory output — even unintentional — creates employer liability.
Risk
Where bias enters
Training data that reflects historical hiring patterns, proxy features (zip code, school name) that correlate with protected class, and feedback loops that reinforce past decisions.
Mitigation
What you can do
Bias audits before deployment, disparate impact testing, human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions, and explainability requirements so decisions can be challenged.
REAL-WORLD PRECEDENT
Amazon (2018): Scrapped an AI recruiting tool after discovering it penalised CVs that contained the word "women's" (e.g. women's chess club) — because it was trained on 10 years of predominantly male hire data. Lesson: A model can be technically accurate on its training distribution and still be systematically biased against protected groups.

Mitigation framework

Pre-deployment bias audit

Measure disparate impact across protected groups before any model goes live. Use standard metrics: selection rate ratios, false positive/negative rates by subgroup.

Human-in-the-loop for consequential decisions

AI shortlisting, performance scoring, and pay recommendations must have a human reviewer who can override. AI should inform, not decide, on protected-class-adjacent outcomes.

Explainability & documentation

Every AI-assisted HR decision needs a documented rationale. NYC LL144 already mandates annual bias audits and candidate disclosure for automated employment tools.

Ongoing monitoring

Bias can drift as the workforce and world change. Continuous monitoring for outcome disparities, with quarterly reviews and a clear trigger for model retraining or removal.

Cisco context

Recruiting automation

Any AI resume screener or interview scorer must be audited before deployment under NYC LL144 if used for NYC-based roles — and Cisco's global commitments likely extend this further.

Performance management

AI-assisted performance scoring must be tested for gender and ethnicity bias across Cisco's 85,000-person workforce before influencing comp, promotions, or PIPs.

Pay equity tools

AI tools that model compensation ranges must be validated for disparate impact on protected classes — especially given Cisco's public pay equity commitments and EEOC scrutiny.

Responsible AI charter

Cisco's existing Responsible AI framework and Ethics team can own the HR bias audit process — aligning AI governance for HR tools with the broader enterprise standard already in place.
