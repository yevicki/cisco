Your #1 Job: Stay Tight

You tend to go on tangents when nervous. Every answer has one job: answer the exact question asked, prove one thing with one story, then stop. The formula below is your guardrail. When you feel yourself drifting, ask: "am I still in R?"

S · Situation → T · Task / Problem → A · Action YOU took → R · Result + What you learned
5-Day Plan
Day 1
Mine Your Stories foundation
  • List every work project + side project you've touched in the last 2 years
  • For each, ask: what was hard? what did YOU specifically do? what changed because of it?
  • Identify at least 6 distinct experiences (you'll map them to themes on Day 2)
  • Write rough bullet notes — don't write full sentences yet
Day 2
Build Your Story Bank core work
  • Map your 6 experiences to the 5 core themes below (some stories cover two themes)
  • Write each story in STAR format — keep S+T to 2-3 sentences max
  • Practice saying each story out loud — it sounds different than it reads
  • Time yourself: target 90 seconds per story, 2 minutes max
Day 3
Cisco Context + Tailoring differentiator
  • Research Cisco's People & Communities / HR AI work — look for public blog posts, LinkedIn posts from the team
  • Prepare your "why Cisco / why this team" answer (2-3 specific reasons, not generic)
  • Tailor at least 2 stories to explicitly mention AI tools, data, or non-technical stakeholders
  • Prepare 3 smart questions for the manager (see section 04)
Day 4
Mock Interview Out Loud most important
  • Do a full 30-min mock — set a timer, answer every question out loud as if it's real
  • Record yourself on your phone — watch it back once, note where you drifted
  • Focus correction on your tangent habit: where did you add detail that wasn't asked for?
  • Use the new chat prompt below to drill with Claude as your mock interviewer
Day 5
Light Review + Rest day before
  • Read through your story bullet points once — don't memorize word-for-word
  • Review your 3 questions for the manager
  • Confirm logistics: time zone, Webex link, camera/mic check
  • Sleep well — preparation is done, trust the work

Likely Questions + How to Answer
👤 About You
Almost Certain to Be Asked
"Tell me about yourself." +
This is not an invitation to recite your resume. The manager wants to know: who are you as an engineer, and why are you here? Structure it in 3 beats: (1) where you are now and what you do, (2) one thing you're proud of or what you're drawn to technically, (3) why this role at Cisco. Keep it under 90 seconds. Don't start with "So I was born in..." or go chronologically through every job.
💡 Draft version: "I'm a full-stack engineer with [X] years of experience, primarily in Java and Angular. Lately I've been focused on [specific area — AI integrations / backend systems / etc.]. What drew me to this role specifically is [1-2 concrete reasons tied to the HR AI platform]. I'm excited to work on software that directly affects how employees experience Cisco."
"Why Cisco? Why this team specifically?" +
This question weeds out people who are just job hunting vs. people who actually care. You need at least one specific reason tied to the HR AI Platform, not Cisco the brand. Research angles: the intersection of AI and HR is genuinely novel and regulated — there's meaningful engineering challenge there. Cisco's scale means the platform affects tens of thousands of employees. If you find any public posts about the team's work, reference them.
⚠️ Avoid: "Cisco is a great company with lots of opportunity." That's a non-answer. The manager has heard it 50 times.
"Where do you see yourself in 2–3 years?" +
As early career, you don't need a 5-year vision. What managers want to hear: you want to grow technically, take on more ownership, and understand the domain deeply. Something like: "In the near term I want to become really solid in [AI integrations / full-stack delivery on this platform]. Longer term I'm interested in taking on more technical leadership — owning larger features end to end. I think HR AI is a space where domain knowledge compounds over time, and I want to build that."
💡 Don't say "I want to be a manager" unless you mean it. At 0-2 years, IC growth is the right answer.
🔨 How You Work
Situational / Behavioral
"Tell me about a time you had to work with a difficult stakeholder or team member." +
Don't throw anyone under the bus. The point of this question is to see if you can navigate conflict with maturity, not to expose who was wrong. Frame it as a misalignment, not a personality clash. Show that you tried to understand their perspective before pushing back. Use your Collaboration story template, but pick an example that had real friction in it.
⚠️ Avoid naming the person or calling them difficult. Say "we had different views on the approach" not "my manager was unreasonable."
"How do you handle feedback or criticism of your work?" +
This tests emotional maturity. The right answer: you welcome it, distinguish between feedback on the work vs. feedback on you personally, and act on it. Give a brief concrete example — a code review comment you disagreed with at first, but updated after understanding the reason. Show that you can be wrong and be fine with it. Early career engineers who can receive feedback gracefully stand out.
"Tell me about a project you're most proud of." +
Pick something where you had meaningful ownership, not just contributed to. It doesn't have to be big — a side project where you made all the decisions is better than a large team project where you wrote one feature. Be specific: what was the technical challenge, what did you decide, what was the outcome. Then connect it to why you care about this work going forward.
💡 If your best example is a side project, lead with it confidently. Side projects show initiative, which matters a lot at the early career level.
"How do you stay current with new technology, especially AI?" +
Be honest and specific — don't say "I read tech blogs" generically. Name what you actually do: specific newsletters (TLDR AI, The Batch), repos you follow on GitHub, things you've built to learn, podcasts, papers you've skimmed. Then tie it to action: "When I learned about RAG, I [built something / read the paper / watched a talk] — here's what I took away." The manager is hiring for an AI platform — showing you're genuinely curious here is a differentiator.
💬 Manager-Specific
High Probability in a Manager Round
"What kind of work environment / management style do you thrive in?" +
This is the manager trying to figure out if they'll enjoy working with you — and if their style will work for you. Be honest but frame it in growth terms: "I do best when I have clear goals but room to figure out the approach. I like managers who give direct feedback — I'd rather know quickly if something isn't working." Avoid: "I like to be left alone" or "I need a lot of structure" at extremes.
"What's something you're actively working to improve?" +
Don't say "I work too hard" or give a strength disguised as a weakness. Pick something real and relevant but not disqualifying — and show you're taking action on it. For example: "I've realized I sometimes jump into solutions before I've fully scoped the problem. I've been working on this by forcing myself to write a one-pager before I open an IDE. It's slowed my start but improved the quality of what I ship."
💡 Your tangent habit is actually a great real answer here — saying "I'm working on being more concise when communicating technical ideas" shows self-awareness and is highly relevant for cross-functional work.
Questions to Ask the Manager

Good questions signal that you've thought seriously about the role. Pick 2-3 of these based on what you genuinely want to know.

What does the first 90 days look like for someone joining this team? Shows you're thinking practically about onboarding, not just getting an offer.
What's the current state of the AI platform — is this greenfield, or are you evolving an existing system? Signals technical curiosity and helps you understand scope.
How does the team balance shipping features with responsible AI practices — things like bias review or explainability? Extremely relevant given the HR AI domain; shows you did your homework.
What does success look like for this role in the first 6 months? Gives you clarity and tells the manager you're results-oriented.
What's something the team has shipped recently that you're proud of? Opens a conversation, shows genuine interest in what they're building.
How does the team stay current on AI developments — is there time carved out for learning or experimentation? Shows you care about growth and is relevant given how fast the AI landscape moves.
